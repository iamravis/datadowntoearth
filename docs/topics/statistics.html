Chapter 1: Introduction

Definition and Importance

Statistics is the study of collecting, analyzing, interpreting, presenting, and organizing data. It is crucial in data science as it provides the tools and methodologies to make sense of complex data, enabling data-driven decision-making and insights.

Role of Statistics in the Data Science Workflow

  • Data Collection: Designing experiments and surveys.
  • Data Cleaning: Handling missing values and outliers.
  • Data Analysis: Summarizing data with descriptive statistics.
  • Data Modeling: Building predictive models using statistical techniques.
  • Data Interpretation: Drawing conclusions and making predictions based on data.

Types of Data

  • Categorical: Data that can be grouped into categories (e.g., gender, nationality).
  • Numerical: Data that represent numbers (e.g., age, salary).
  • Ordinal: Data with a meaningful order but no fixed intervals (e.g., ranks).
  • Nominal: Data without a meaningful order (e.g., colors).

Scales of Measurement

  • Nominal: Categories without a specific order.
  • Ordinal: Ordered categories.
  • Interval: Ordered categories with equal intervals but no true zero (e.g., temperature in Celsius).
  • Ratio: Ordered categories with equal intervals and a true zero (e.g., height, weight).

Chapter 2: Fundamentals of Descriptive Statistics

Measures of Central Tendency

  • Mean
    • Arithmetic Mean: Sum of values divided by the number of values.
    • Geometric Mean: nth root of the product of n values.
    • Harmonic Mean: Reciprocal of the average of the reciprocals of the values.
  • Median: The middle value when data is ordered.
  • Mode: The most frequently occurring value in the data set.

Measures of Dispersion

  • Variance: Average of the squared differences from the mean.
  • Standard Deviation: Square root of the variance.
  • Range: Difference between the maximum and minimum values.
  • Interquartile Range: Difference between the 75th and 25th percentiles.
  • Coefficient of Variation: Standard deviation divided by the mean.
  • Skewness: Measure of the asymmetry of the data distribution.
  • Kurtosis: Measure of the “tailedness” of the data distribution.
  • Percentiles and Quartiles: Values below which a certain percentage of data falls.
  • Box Plots and Whisker Diagrams: Visual representations of data dispersion.

Chapter 3: Data Visualization

  • Histograms: Show the distribution of numerical data.
  • Scatter Plots: Show relationships between two numerical variables.
  • Bar Charts and Pie Charts: Represent categorical data.
  • Heat Maps: Visualize data through variations in color.
  • Q-Q Plots: Compare the distribution of a dataset with a theoretical distribution.
  • Violin Plots: Combine box plots and density plots to show data distribution.

Chapter 4: Probability Theory

Basic Probability Concepts

  • Sample Space and Events: The set of all possible outcomes and specific outcomes of interest.
  • Axioms of Probability: Fundamental rules that probabilities must follow.
  • Mutually Exclusive and Independent Events: Events that cannot happen simultaneously and events whose occurrence does not affect each other.

Probability Distributions

  • Discrete Distributions: Bernoulli, Binomial, Poisson, Geometric.
  • Continuous Distributions: Normal, Exponential, Uniform, Student’s t.
  • Joint, Marginal, and Conditional Probabilities: Probabilities involving two or more variables.

Important Theorems

  • Bayes’ Theorem: Relates conditional probabilities.
  • Law of Total Probability: Computes the total probability of an outcome.
  • Expectation and Variance of Random Variables: Measures the central tendency and dispersion of random variables.

Chapter 5: Inferential Statistics

Population vs. Sample

Understanding the difference between the entire population and a subset of it (sample).

Sampling Methods

  • Simple Random Sampling: Every member has an equal chance of being selected.
  • Stratified Sampling: Population is divided into strata, and samples are taken from each.
  • Cluster Sampling: Population is divided into clusters, and entire clusters are sampled.
  • Systematic Sampling: Every nth member is selected from a list.

Sampling Distributions and Central Limit Theorem

  • Sampling Distributions: Distributions of sample statistics.
  • Central Limit Theorem: The distribution of sample means approaches a normal distribution as the sample size grows.

Confidence Intervals

  • For Means, Proportions, and Variances: Intervals within which the population parameter is expected to lie with a certain confidence level.
  • One-Sample and Two-Sample Cases: Comparisons within and between samples.

Hypothesis Testing

  • Null and Alternative Hypotheses: Statements to be tested.
  • Type I and Type II Errors: False positives and false negatives.
  • Power of a Test: Probability of correctly rejecting the null hypothesis.
  • p-values and Statistical Significance: Measures the strength of evidence against the null hypothesis.
  • Effect Size and Practical Significance: Magnitude and real-world importance of results.

Chapter 6: Regression Analysis

Simple Linear Regression

  • Least Squares Estimation: Method to find the best-fit line.
  • Assumptions of Linear Regression: Linearity, independence, homoscedasticity, normality.
  • R-squared and Adjusted R-squared: Measures of model fit.

Multiple Linear Regression

  • Multicollinearity: High correlation between independent variables.
  • Interaction Terms: Representing combined effects of variables.
  • Dummy Variables: Representing categorical variables.

Logistic Regression

  • Odds Ratios: Measure of association between variables.
  • Maximum Likelihood Estimation: Method to estimate parameters.

Other Types of Regression

  • Polynomial Regression: Fits a polynomial equation to the data.
  • Generalized Linear Models (GLMs): Extends linear models to non-normal data.
  • Non-linear Regression: Models non-linear relationships.

Chapter 7: Analysis of Variance (ANOVA)

  • One-way ANOVA: Tests differences between means of three or more groups.
  • Two-way ANOVA: Examines the influence of two factors on the dependent variable.
  • MANOVA: Multivariate analysis of variance.
  • Repeated Measures ANOVA: For repeated measurements of the same subjects.
  • Mixed-effects Models: Combine fixed and random effects.
