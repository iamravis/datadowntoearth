Data Collection and Management in Maternity Care

Electronic Patient Record (EPR) Systems

Overview

EPR systems are crucial in modern healthcare for improving patient care, enhancing data accuracy, and facilitating communication among healthcare providers. These systems have evolved from basic digital repositories to comprehensive platforms integrating various functionalities, such as patient tracking, medication management, and clinical decision support. Key features of EPR systems include patient data management, electronic prescribing, and integration with diagnostic tools.

Example: A hospital’s EPR system allows doctors to access a patient’s entire medical history, including previous admissions, current medications, and recent test results, all from a single interface.

OUH’s EPR System

Oxford University Hospitals (OUH) utilises a specialised EPR system tailored for maternity care. It includes modules for:

  • Antenatal Care: Captures data on initial assessments, routine check-ups, and risk assessments.
  • Labour and Delivery: Records contraction patterns, fetal monitoring, and delivery outcomes.
  • Postnatal Care: Tracks maternal recovery and early infant care.

These modules ensure comprehensive and seamless care throughout the maternity journey.

Example: During a routine antenatal visit, a midwife enters the patient’s blood pressure, weight, and results of a glucose tolerance test into the EPR system. This information is instantly available to the obstetrician for review.

Integration

The EPR system integrates with other hospital systems such as:

  • Laboratory Information Systems: Facilitates direct access to test results.
  • Radiology: Allows for the integration of imaging data.
  • Pharmacy: Manages medication orders and administration. This integration enhances the efficiency and quality of patient care by ensuring that all relevant data is easily accessible.

Example: When a blood test is ordered, the results are automatically uploaded to the EPR, where they can be reviewed alongside the patient’s clinical notes and medication history.

Maternity Data Types

Antenatal Records

Data collected during pregnancy includes:

  • Medical History: Chronic conditions, previous pregnancies, and surgeries.
  • Risk Assessments: Genetic screenings, lifestyle factors, and environmental exposures.
  • Routine Check-ups: Blood pressure, weight, fetal growth, and maternal well-being.

Example: A patient’s antenatal record might show a history of gestational diabetes, a current risk assessment for preeclampsia, and routine check-up data indicating normal fetal growth.

Labour and Delivery Data

Critical data points during labour and delivery encompass:

  • Contraction Patterns: Frequency, duration, and intensity of contractions.
  • Fetal Monitoring: Heart rate patterns and distress indicators.
  • Delivery Outcomes: Type of delivery, complications, and Apgar scores.

Example: During labour, the EPR system records the frequency of contractions and displays the fetal heart rate in real-time, allowing the healthcare team to respond quickly to any signs of distress.

Postpartum Care Information

Post-delivery data includes:

  • Maternal Recovery: Monitoring of bleeding, pain levels, and healing of any incisions.
  • Early Infant Care: Feeding, weight checks, and initial health assessments.

Example: In the postpartum ward, a nurse enters the mother’s pain levels and bleeding observations into the EPR system, along with the newborn’s weight and feeding patterns.

Neonatal Data

Specific data for newborns includes:

  • Apgar Scores: Assessment of the newborn’s physical condition at 1 and 5 minutes after birth.
  • Birth Weight: Measurement of the newborn’s weight.
  • Initial Health Assessments: Screening for congenital conditions and early signs of health issues.

Example: Immediately after birth, a newborn is given an Apgar score, which is recorded in the EPR alongside their birth weight and results of a hearing test.

Data Quality Control

Consistency Checks

Ensuring data coherence involves:

  • Gestational Age Verification: Checking that recorded dates for conception and delivery are logical.
  • Cross-Referencing Data: Ensuring consistency across different records and modules.

Example: A system alert is triggered if the recorded gestational age at delivery is inconsistent with the date of the last menstrual period.

Range Validation

Setting clinically appropriate ranges for parameters such as:

  • Blood Pressure: Ensuring readings fall within normal ranges for pregnant women.
  • Fetal Heart Rate: Monitoring rates to detect any abnormalities.

Example: The system flags a blood pressure reading of 150/100 mmHg as outside the normal range, prompting a review by the healthcare provider.

Completeness Assessment

Methods to ensure all critical fields are filled include:

  • Identifying Essential Fields: Highlighting mandatory data points.
  • Completeness Thresholds: Setting minimum data entry standards.

Example: During data entry, mandatory fields such as ‘date of last menstrual period’ and ‘estimated due date’ must be filled before the record can be saved.

Error Detection and Correction

Strategies for identifying and correcting errors:

  • Classification of Errors: Categorising errors based on their type and severity.
  • Correction Workflows: Establishing protocols for rectifying identified issues.

Example: An error is flagged when a patient’s height is entered as 150 cm in one visit and 160 cm in another. The discrepancy is reviewed and corrected by a data manager.
