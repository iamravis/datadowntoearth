Chapter 12: Generative Modeling For Audio And Music

Generative modeling for audio and music involves creating synthetic audio signals, music, and speech that maintain the statistical properties and temporal structure of real-world audio. This field has applications in music composition, sound design, speech synthesis, and audio enhancement.

Modeling Audio Signals and Waveforms

Audio signals are typically represented as time-series data, with amplitudes varying over time. The challenge in modeling audio is capturing both the short-term and long-term dependencies in the signal.

Time-Domain Representation

In the time domain, an audio signal \(x(t)\) is represented as a function of time:

\[ x(t) = A(t) \sin(2\pi f t + \phi(t)) \]

Where \(A(t)\) is the time-varying amplitude, \(f\) is the frequency, and \(\phi(t)\) is the phase.

Frequency-Domain Representation

The frequency-domain representation is obtained using the Fourier Transform:

\[ X(f) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(t) e^{-j2\pi ft} dt \]

This representation captures the frequency components of the signal but loses temporal information.

Time-Frequency Representations

Time-frequency representations, such as the Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT), capture both temporal and spectral information:

\[ STFT\{x(t)\}(\tau, f) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(t) w(t-\tau) e^{-j2\pi ft} dt \]

Where \(w(t)\) is a window function. The STFT provides a spectrogram, which is a 2D representation of the signal’s frequency content over time.

Wavelet Transform

The Wavelet Transform provides a multi-resolution analysis of the signal, capturing both high and low-frequency components at different scales:

\[ W_x(a, b) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(t) \psi^* \left( \frac{t - b}{a} \right) dt \]

Where \(\psi(t)\) is the mother wavelet, \(a\) is the scale parameter, and \(b\) is the translation parameter.

Generative Adversarial Networks for Music Generation

GANs have been adapted for music generation, addressing challenges such as temporal coherence and multi-track generation.

MidiNet

MidiNet is a GAN-based model for generating symbolic music representations (MIDI).

Key Features:

  1. Convolutional Architecture: Both the generator and discriminator use convolutional layers to process the MIDI-like representations.
  2. Conditional Generation: The generator is conditioned on chords or melodies to produce coherent musical pieces.

Generator:

The generator takes a latent vector \(z\) and conditioning information \(c\) to produce a MIDI-like representation:

\[ G(z, c) = M, \quad M \in \{0, 1\}^{T \times P} \]

Where \(T\) is the number of time steps and \(P\) is the number of pitches.

Discriminator:

The discriminator evaluates the authenticity of the generated music:

\[ D(M, c) \in [0, 1] \]

MuseGAN

MuseGAN extends the GAN framework to generate multi-track music.

Key Features:

  1. Multi-Stream Architecture: Separate streams for different instrument tracks, allowing for polyphonic music generation.
  2. Temporal Structure Modeling: Uses binary-valued piano-rolls to represent the presence or absence of notes.

Generator:

The generator consists of multiple sub-generators, one for each track:

\[ G_i(z_i, c) = M_i, \quad i = 1, ..., N \]

Where \(N\) is the number of tracks.

The full multi-track music is generated as:

\[ M = [M_1, M_2, ..., M_N] \]

Discriminator:

The discriminator evaluates the multi-track music:

\[ D(M, c) \in [0, 1] \]

Autoregressive Models for Audio Synthesis

Autoregressive models generate audio samples sequentially, conditioning each sample on previous samples.

WaveNet

WaveNet is a deep neural network for generating raw audio waveforms.

Key Features:

  1. Dilated Causal Convolutions: Allow the model to have a large receptive field, capturing long-term dependencies without increasing the number of parameters significantly.
  2. Probabilistic Output: Predicts the probability distribution of the next sample given previous samples.

Model Architecture:

The WaveNet model predicts the probability distribution of the next sample \(x_t\) given all previous samples:

\[ p(x_t | x_1, ..., x_{t-1}) = f_\theta(x_{t-1}, ..., x_{t-R}) \]

Where \(R\) is the receptive field size and \(f_\theta\) is a neural network with parameters \(\theta\).

The joint probability of a waveform is factorized as:

\[ p(x) = \prod_{t=1}^T p(x_t | x_1, ..., x_{t-1}) \]

Training:

WaveNet is trained to maximize the likelihood of the training data:

\[ \mathcal{L}(\theta) = \sum_{t=1}^T \log p(x_t | x_1, ..., x_{t-1}; \theta) \]

Tacotron

Tacotron is an end-to-end text-to-speech synthesis model that converts text to speech.

Key Components:

  1. Encoder: Converts input text into a sequence of hidden states.
  2. Attention Mechanism: Aligns the text sequence with the audio sequence.
  3. Decoder: Generates mel-spectrogram frames from the aligned text sequence.
  4. Post-Processing Net: Converts mel-spectrograms to waveforms.

Attention Mechanism:

The attention mechanism computes alignment probabilities between the encoder states and the decoder states:

\[ \alpha_{i,j} = \text{softmax}(e_{i,j}) \]

\[ e_{i,j} = v^T \tanh(W h_j + V s_i + b) \]

Where \(h_j\) is the encoder state, \(s_i\) is the decoder state, and \(W\), \(V\), \(v\), and \(b\) are learnable parameters.

Training:

Tacotron is trained to minimize the mean squared error between the predicted and target mel-spectrogram frames:

\[ \mathcal{L}(\theta) = \sum_{i=1}^N ||y_i - \hat{y}_i||_2^2 \]

Where \(y_i\) is the target mel-spectrogram frame and \(\hat{y}_i\) is the predicted frame.

Text-to-Speech and Speech Enhancement

Text-to-Speech (TTS) systems convert written text into spoken audio, while speech enhancement aims to improve the quality of speech signals.

Text-to-Speech

Modern TTS systems often use neural networks to map text to speech. The process typically involves:

  1. Text Analysis: Convert text to phonemes or other linguistic features.
  2. Acoustic Model: Map linguistic features to acoustic features (e.g., mel-spectrograms).
  3. Vocoder: Convert acoustic features to waveforms.

Acoustic Model:

The acoustic model can be formulated as:

\[ y = f_\theta(x) \]

Where \(x\) is the input text (or linguistic features) and \(y\) is the output acoustic features.

Vocoder:

The vocoder converts the acoustic features to waveforms. Common vocoders include Griffin-Lim, WaveNet, and WaveGlow.

Speech Enhancement

Speech enhancement techniques aim to improve the quality of speech signals by reducing noise and other distortions.

Denoising Autoencoders:

A denoising autoencoder is trained to reconstruct clean speech from noisy input:

\[ \hat{s} = f_\theta(x) \]

Where \(x\) is the noisy input signal and \(\hat{s}\) is the estimated clean speech signal.

Spectral Subtraction:

Spectral subtraction estimates the noise spectrum and subtracts it from the noisy speech spectrum:

\[ \hat{S}(f) = X(f) - \hat{N}(f) \]

Where \(X(f)\) is the noisy speech spectrum and \(\hat{N}(f)\) is the estimated noise spectrum.

Techniques for Handling Long-Range Dependencies and Structure in Music

Capturing long-range dependencies and structural elements in music is crucial for generating coherent and musically meaningful compositions.

Hierarchical Models

Hierarchical models capture music structure at different time scales:

  1. Bar-Level Structure: Captures the structure within a single bar.
  2. Phrase-Level Structure: Captures the structure across multiple bars.
  3. Section-Level Structure: Captures the structure across multiple phrases.

A hierarchical model can be formulated as:

\[ p(x) = p(x_{section}) \prod_i p(x_{phrase_i} | x_{section}) \prod_j p(x_{bar_j} | x_{phrase_i}) \prod_k p(x_{note_k} | x_{bar_j}) \]

Attention Mechanisms

Attention mechanisms allow models to focus on relevant parts of the input when generating each output. In music generation, this can help capture long-range dependencies:

\[ \text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V \]

Where \(Q\), \(K\), and \(V\) are query, key, and value matrices derived from the input.

Memory Networks

Memory networks augment neural networks with an external memory that can be read from and written to. This allows the model to store and retrieve information over long time spans:

\[ m_t = \text{Update}(m_{t-1}, x_t) \]

\[ r_t = \text{Read}(m_t, q_t) \]

Where \(m_t\) is the memory state, \(x_t\) is the input, \(q_t\) is a query, and \(r_t\) is the retrieved information.

Transformer-based Models

Transformer-based models have shown promising results in capturing long-range dependencies in music. The self-attention mechanism allows the model to attend to any part of the input sequence:

\[ \text{MultiHead}(Q, K, V) = \text{Concat}(head_1, ..., head_h)W^O \]

Where each head is computed as:

\[ head_i = \text{Attention}(QW_i^Q, KW_i^K, VW_i^V) \]

Music Transformer

The Music Transformer adapts the Transformer architecture for music generation, capturing long-term dependencies and structure:

\[ \text{MusicTransformer}(x) = \text{Decoder}(\text{Encoder}(x)) \]

Where the encoder and decoder use self-attention to process the input sequence.

Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs)

RNNs, including LSTMs and GRUs, are commonly used to model sequential data in music generation. They capture dependencies by maintaining a hidden state that evolves over time:

\[ h_t = \sigma(W_h h_{t-1} + W_x x_t + b) \]

Variational Autoencoders (VAEs)

VAEs can be used to generate music by learning a latent representation of the musical structure:

\[ z \sim q_\phi(z|x) \]

\[ x \sim p_\theta(x|z) \]

Where \(q_\phi(z|x)\) is the encoder and \(p_\theta(x|z)\) is the decoder.

Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs)

GANs can generate music by learning to produce realistic samples that are indistinguishable from real music:

\[ G(z) = x, \quad D(x) \in [0, 1] \]

Where \(G\) is the generator and \(D\) is the discriminator.

Reinforcement Learning

Reinforcement learning can be used to optimize music generation models based on specific musical criteria:

\[ \mathcal{L}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_\theta} [R(x)] \]

Where \(R(x)\) is a reward function that evaluates the quality of the generated music.

These techniques enable the generation of music with coherent long-term structure and dependencies, addressing one of the key challenges in music generation.
