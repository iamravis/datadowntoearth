Chapter 11: Evaluation And Metrics

Evaluating the quality and performance of generative models is a complex task due to the diverse nature of generated data and the various aspects that need to be assessed. This comprehensive overview covers a wide range of evaluation metrics and techniques used in the field of generative modeling.

Inception Score and Fréchet Inception Distance (FID)

Inception Score (IS)

The Inception Score is a widely used metric for evaluating the quality and diversity of generated images, particularly in the context of GANs.

Calculation:

  1. Pass the generated images through a pre-trained Inception v3 network to obtain the conditional label distribution \(p(y|x)\).
  2. Calculate the marginal label distribution \(p(y)\) by averaging \(p(y|x)\) over all generated images.
  3. Compute the KL divergence between \(p(y|x)\) and \(p(y)\) for each image and take the exponential of the average KL divergence:

\[ IS = \exp \left( \mathbb{E}_x \left[ D_{KL}(p(y|x) || p(y)) \right] \right) \]

Interpretation:

  • Higher IS indicates better quality and diversity of generated images.
  • The score ranges from 1 to the number of classes in the Inception model (1000 for ImageNet).

Limitations:

  • Sensitive to the choice of pre-trained model
  • May not capture all aspects of image quality and diversity
  • Can be insensitive to certain types of mode collapse

Fréchet Inception Distance (FID)

FID measures the similarity between the distributions of real and generated images by comparing their feature representations.

Calculation:

  1. Pass both real and generated images through the Inception v3 network to obtain feature representations.
  2. Compute the mean and covariance of the feature representations for real images \((\mu_r, \Sigma_r)\) and generated images \((\mu_g, \Sigma_g)\).
  3. Calculate the Fréchet distance between the two distributions:

\[ FID = ||\mu_r - \mu_g||^2 + \text{Tr}(\Sigma_r + \Sigma_g - 2(\Sigma_r \Sigma_g)^{1/2}) \]

Interpretation:

  • Lower FID indicates that the generated images are more similar to the real images.
  • FID is more sensitive to the overall statistics of the generated images than IS.

Advantages:

  • Captures both quality and diversity
  • More robust to mode collapse than IS
  • Correlates well with human judgment of image quality

Limitations:

  • Assumes that feature representations follow a multivariate Gaussian distribution
  • Sensitive to the choice of feature extractor

Perceptual Similarity Metrics

Learned Perceptual Image Patch Similarity (LPIPS)

LPIPS measures the perceptual similarity between images by comparing their feature representations obtained from deep neural networks.

Calculation:

  1. Pass both images through a pre-trained network (e.g., VGG, AlexNet) to obtain feature representations at multiple layers.
  2. Compute the L2 distance between the feature representations for each layer.
  3. Average the distances across layers, weighted by learned weights:

\[ LPIPS(x, y) = \sum_l w_l \frac{1}{H_lW_l} \sum_{h,w} ||f_l^{hw}(x) - f_l^{hw}(y)||_2^2 \]

Where: - \(f_l^{hw}(x)\) is the feature vector at position \((h,w)\) in layer \(l\) for image \(x\) - \(H_l\) and \(W_l\) are the height and width of the feature map in layer \(l\) - \(w_l\) are learned weights for each layer

Advantages:

  • Correlates well with human perception of image similarity
  • Can be fine-tuned for specific domains or tasks

Limitations:

  • Requires a pre-trained network
  • May not capture all aspects of perceptual similarity

Structural Similarity Index (SSIM)

SSIM measures the structural similarity between images by comparing their luminance, contrast, and structure.

Calculation:

  1. Compute the luminance, contrast, and structure comparisons:

\[ l(x, y) = \frac{2\mu_x \mu_y + C_1}{\mu_x^2 + \mu_y^2 + C_1} \]

\[ c(x, y) = \frac{2\sigma_x \sigma_y + C_2}{\sigma_x^2 + \sigma_y^2 + C_2} \]

\[ s(x, y) = \frac{\sigma_{xy} + C_3}{\sigma_x \sigma_y + C_3} \]

  1. Combine the comparisons to obtain the SSIM score:

\[ SSIM(x, y) = l(x, y)^\alpha \cdot c(x, y)^\beta \cdot s(x, y)^\gamma \]

Where: - \(\mu_x\) and \(\mu_y\) are the means of images \(x\) and \(y\) - \(\sigma_x\) and \(\sigma_y\) are the standard deviations of images \(x\) and \(y\) - \(\sigma_{xy}\) is the covariance of images \(x\) and \(y\) - \(C_1\), \(C_2\), and \(C_3\) are constants to stabilize the division - \(\alpha\), \(\beta\), and \(\gamma\) are weights for each component (typically set to 1)

Advantages:

  • Does not require a pre-trained network
  • Captures structural information in images

Limitations:

  • May not align perfectly with human perception in all cases
  • Sensitive to small geometric transformations

Downstream Task Performance and Human Evaluation

Downstream Task Performance

Evaluating generative models based on their performance on downstream tasks involves training models on the generated data and assessing their performance on specific tasks.

Classification Performance:

  1. Train a classifier on generated data:

    \[ \hat{y} = f_\theta(x), \quad x \sim p_g \]

  2. Evaluate the classifier’s accuracy on real data:

    \[ \text{Accuracy} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N \mathbb{I}(\hat{y}_i = y_i), \quad (x_i, y_i) \sim p_{data} \]

Regression Performance:

  1. Train a regressor on generated data:

    \[ \hat{y} = f_\theta(x), \quad x \sim p_g \]

  2. Evaluate the regressor’s mean squared error (MSE) on real data:

    \[ \text{MSE} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2, \quad (x_i, y_i) \sim p_{data} \]

Advantages:

  • Directly measures the utility of generated data for specific tasks
  • Can reveal issues not captured by other metrics

Limitations:

  • Performance depends on the choice of downstream task and model
  • May not capture all aspects of data quality and diversity

Human Evaluation

Human evaluation involves subjective assessment of the quality and realism of generated data by human judges.

Mean Opinion Score (MOS):

  1. Human judges rate the quality of generated samples on a scale (e.g., 1 to 5).

  2. Compute the average score:

    \[ MOS = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N \text{Score}_i \]

Pairwise Comparison:

  1. Human judges compare pairs of generated and real samples.

  2. Compute the preference score:

    \[ \text{Preference Score} = \frac{\text{Number of times generated preferred}}{\text{Total number of comparisons}} \]

Advantages:

  • Directly captures human perception of quality and realism
  • Can reveal issues not captured by automated metrics

Limitations:

  • Time-consuming and expensive
  • Subject to individual biases and inconsistencies

Mode Collapse and Mode Dropping

Mode Collapse

Mode collapse occurs when the generator produces a limited variety of samples, failing to capture the diversity of the real data distribution.

Detection Methods:

  1. Visual Inspection: Manually inspect generated samples for diversity.

  2. Cluster Analysis:

    1. Cluster generated samples using k-means or other clustering algorithms.
    2. Compare the number and size of clusters to those of real data.

    \[ \text{Cluster Ratio} = \frac{\text{Number of Clusters in Generated Data}}{\text{Number of Clusters in Real Data}} \]

  3. Birthday Paradox Test:

    1. Generate N samples and count unique samples (U).
    2. Compare U to the expected number of unique samples in a truly diverse set.

    \[ \text{Diversity Score} = \frac{U}{\text{Expected U for diverse set}} \]

Mitigation Techniques:

  1. Minibatch Discrimination: Introduce a layer in the discriminator that compares samples within a minibatch to encourage diversity.

    \[ D(x) = f(x, M(x)) \]

    Where \(M(x)\) computes statistics across the minibatch.

  2. Feature Matching: Match the intermediate feature representations of real and generated samples.

    \[ \mathcal{L}_{FM} = ||\mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_{data}} f(x) - \mathbb{E}_{z \sim p_z} f(G(z))||_2 \]

  3. Unrolled GANs: Update the generator using gradients from multiple discriminator updates.

Mode Dropping

Mode dropping occurs when the generator fails to produce samples from certain modes of the real data distribution.

Detection Methods:

  1. Coverage Metrics: Measure the coverage of generated samples over the real data distribution.

    \[ \text{Coverage} = \frac{\text{Number of Real Data Modes Covered}}{\text{Total Number of Real Data Modes}} \]

  2. Precision and Recall: Evaluate the precision and recall of generated samples with respect to real data (discussed in detail in the next section).

Mitigation Techniques:

  1. Regularized GANs: Add regularization terms to encourage mode coverage.
  2. Mixture of Generators: Use multiple generators to capture different modes of the data distribution.

Precision and Recall for Generative Models

Precision and recall metrics adapted for generative models measure the fidelity and diversity of generated samples.

Precision

Precision measures the proportion of generated samples that are realistic and similar to real data.

\[ \text{Precision} = \frac{\text{Number of Realistic Generated Samples}}{\text{Total Number of Generated Samples}} \]

Recall

Recall measures the proportion of real data distribution that is covered by the generated samples.

\[ \text{Recall} = \frac{\text{Number of Real Data Modes Covered by Generated Samples}}{\text{Total Number of Real Data Modes}} \]

F1 Score

The F1 score combines precision and recall into a single metric:

\[ F1 = 2 \cdot \frac{\text{Precision} \cdot \text{Recall}}{\text{Precision} + \text{Recall}} \]

Improved Precision and Recall Estimation

A more sophisticated approach to estimating precision and recall for generative models involves:

  1. Feature Extraction: Extract feature representations of real and generated samples using a pre-trained network.

  2. Manifold Estimation: Estimate the manifolds of real and generated data in the feature space.

  3. Precision Calculation:

    \[ \text{Precision} = \frac{1}{N_g} \sum_{i=1}^{N_g} \mathbb{I}(\min_{j} d(g_i, r_j) < \epsilon) \]

  4. Recall Calculation:

    \[ \text{Recall} = \frac{1}{N_r} \sum_{j=1}^{N_r} \mathbb{I}(\min_{i} d(r_j, g_i) < \epsilon) \]

Where: - \(N_g\) and \(N_r\) are the number of generated and real samples - \(g_i\) and \(r_j\) are the feature representations of generated and real samples - \(d(\cdot, \cdot)\) is the distance metric in the feature space - \(\epsilon\) is a threshold for determining similarity

Density and Coverage (DC) Metrics

An extension of precision and recall that addresses some limitations:

  1. Density: Measures the fraction of generated samples that are close to real samples.

    \[ \text{Density} = \frac{1}{N_g} \sum_{i=1}^{N_g} \mathbb{I}(\min_{j} d(g_i, r_j) < \epsilon) \]

  2. Coverage: Measures the fraction of real samples that have a generated sample nearby.

    \[ \text{Coverage} = \frac{1}{N_r} \sum_{j=1}^{N_r} \mathbb{I}(\min_{i} d(r_j, g_i) < \epsilon) \]

These metrics provide a more nuanced evaluation of the quality and diversity of generated samples, helping to identify issues such as mode collapse and mode dropping.

Conclusion

Evaluating generative models is a complex task that requires a combination of quantitative metrics and qualitative assessments. The metrics and techniques discussed in this overview provide a comprehensive toolkit for assessing various aspects of generative model performance, including visual quality, diversity, fidelity, and utility for downstream tasks. As the field of generative modeling continues to advance, new evaluation methods are likely to emerge, addressing the limitations of current approaches and providing even more accurate and meaningful assessments of generative model performance.
