Chapter 13: Diffusion Models

Diffusion models are a class of generative models that have gained significant attention in recent years due to their ability to produce high-quality samples across various domains, including images, audio, and text. These models are based on the principle of gradually adding noise to data and then learning to reverse this process.

1. Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPMs)

DDPMs are the foundation of many modern diffusion models. They work by gradually adding noise to data and then learning to reverse this process.

1.1 Forward Process

The forward process, also known as the diffusion process, gradually adds Gaussian noise to the data over T steps:

\[ q(x_t | x_{t-1}) = \mathcal{N}(x_t; \sqrt{1 - \beta_t} x_{t-1}, \beta_t I) \]

Where: - \(x_t\) is the noisy version of the data at step t - \(\beta_t\) is the noise schedule, typically increasing from \(\beta_1\) to \(\beta_T\) - \(\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)\) denotes a Gaussian distribution with mean \(\mu\) and variance \(\sigma^2\)

1.1.1 Noise Schedules

Different noise schedules have been proposed:

  1. Linear schedule: \(\beta_t = \beta_1 + (\beta_T - \beta_1) \frac{t-1}{T-1}\)
  2. Cosine schedule: \(\beta_t = 1 - \frac{\cos(\frac{\pi t}{2T})}{\cos(\frac{\pi (t-1)}{2T})}\)
  3. Sigmoid schedule: \(\beta_t = \beta_1 + (\beta_T - \beta_1) \text{sigmoid}(\frac{t-1}{T-1})\)

1.1.2 Reparameterization Trick

The forward process can be reparameterized as:

\[ x_t = \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}x_0 + \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}\epsilon \]

Where \(\bar{\alpha}_t = \prod_{s=1}^t (1 - \beta_s)\) and \(\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)\)

1.2 Reverse Process

The reverse process, also known as the denoising process, aims to gradually remove the noise:

\[ p_\theta(x_{t-1} | x_t) = \mathcal{N}(x_{t-1}; \mu_\theta(x_t, t), \Sigma_\theta(x_t, t)) \]

1.2.1 Parameterization of the Reverse Process

In practice, instead of directly predicting \(\mu_\theta\) and \(\Sigma_\theta\), most models predict the noise \(\epsilon_\theta\):

\[ \epsilon_\theta(x_t, t) \approx \epsilon \]

The mean of the reverse process can then be computed as:

\[ \mu_\theta(x_t, t) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-\beta_t}}(x_t - \frac{\beta_t}{\sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}}\epsilon_\theta(x_t, t)) \]

1.2.2 Variance Prediction

Some models also predict the variance:

\[ \Sigma_\theta(x_t, t) = \exp(v_\theta(x_t, t)) \cdot \tilde{\beta}_t \]

Where \(\tilde{\beta}_t\) is a fixed or learned base variance.

1.3 Training Objective

The model is trained to minimize the negative log-likelihood:

\[ L = \mathbb{E}_{q(x_0)}[-\log p_\theta(x_0)] \]

1.3.1 Variational Lower Bound

This is typically approximated using a variational lower bound:

\[ L = \mathbb{E}_{q(x_{0:T})}[\log \frac{q(x_{1:T}|x_0)}{p_\theta(x_{0:T})}] \]

1.3.2 Simplified Objective

In practice, this is often further simplified to a reweighted objective:

\[ L_{\text{simple}} = \mathbb{E}_{t, x_0, \epsilon}[||\epsilon - \epsilon_\theta(x_t, t)||^2] \]

1.3.3 Importance Sampling

Some works propose using importance sampling to improve the training objective:

\[ L_{\text{IS}} = \mathbb{E}_{t, x_0, \epsilon}[w(t)||\epsilon - \epsilon_\theta(x_t, t)||^2] \]

Where \(w(t)\) is an importance weight.

2. Sampling from Diffusion Models

2.1 DDPM (Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models)

DDPM sampling follows the reverse process:

  1. Sample \(x_T \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)\)
  2. For t = T to 1: \[ x_{t-1} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - \beta_t}}(x_t - \frac{\beta_t}{\sqrt{1 - \bar{\beta_t}}}\epsilon_\theta(x_t, t)) + \sigma_t z \] Where \(z \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)\) and \(\sigma_t^2 = \beta_t\)

2.1.1 Ancestral Sampling

Ancestral sampling is the standard approach used in DDPMs:

\[ x_{t-1} \sim p_\theta(x_{t-1}|x_t) \]

2.1.2 Truncated Sampling

To speed up sampling, some methods propose truncating the sampling process:

\[ x_{T'} \sim q(x_{T'}|x_0), \quad T' < T \]

2.2 DDIM (Denoising Diffusion Implicit Models)

DDIM introduces a non-Markovian sampling process that allows for faster sampling:

\[ x_{t-1} = \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_{t-1}} \left(\frac{x_t - \sqrt{1 - \bar{\alpha}_t}\epsilon_\theta(x_t, t)}{\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}}\right) + \sqrt{1 - \bar{\alpha}_{t-1} - \sigma_t^2}\epsilon_\theta(x_t, t) + \sigma_t \epsilon \]

2.2.1 Deterministic Sampling

DDIM allows for deterministic sampling when \(\sigma_t = 0\):

\[ x_{t-1} = \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_{t-1}} \left(\frac{x_t - \sqrt{1 - \bar{\alpha}_t}\epsilon_\theta(x_t, t)}{\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}}\right) + \sqrt{1 - \bar{\alpha}_{t-1}}\epsilon_\theta(x_t, t) \]

2.2.2 Stochastic Sampling

Stochastic sampling in DDIM is achieved by setting \(\sigma_t > 0\):

\[ \sigma_t^2 = \eta \cdot \tilde{\beta}_t \]

Where \(\eta \in [0, 1]\) controls the level of stochasticity.

3. Applications in Image Generation

3.1 Latent Diffusion

Latent Diffusion models apply diffusion in a compressed latent space:

  1. Train an autoencoder to compress images to a latent space: \[ z = E(x), \quad \hat{x} = D(z) \]
  2. Apply diffusion in this latent space: \[ q(z_t | z_{t-1}) = \mathcal{N}(z_t; \sqrt{1 - \beta_t} z_{t-1}, \beta_t I) \]
  3. Use the decoder to generate high-resolution images: \[ x = D(z_0) \]

3.1.1 Perceptual Compression

The autoencoder is often trained with a perceptual loss:

\[ L_{\text{AE}} = ||x - \hat{x}||_2^2 + \lambda L_{\text{perceptual}}(x, \hat{x}) \]

3.1.2 Cross-Attention Conditioning

For conditional generation, cross-attention layers are added to the U-Net:

\[ \text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V \]

Where \(Q\) is derived from the latent representation and \(K, V\) are derived from the conditioning information.

3.2 Imagen

Imagen is a text-to-image diffusion model that uses:

  1. A large language model (e.g., T5-XXL) for text understanding: \[ e = LM(text) \]
  2. A cascade of diffusion models for image generation at increasing resolutions: \[ x_1 = DM_1(e), \quad x_2 = DM_2(x_1, e), \quad x_3 = DM_3(x_2, e) \]

3.2.1 Classifier-Free Guidance

Imagen uses classifier-free guidance for better text-image alignment:

\[ \epsilon_\theta(x_t, t, e) = (1 + w) \epsilon_\theta(x_t, t, e) - w \epsilon_\theta(x_t, t) \]

3.2.2 Dynamic Thresholding

To improve sample quality, Imagen employs dynamic thresholding:

\[ x_t = \text{clip}(x_t, -s \cdot \text{percentile}(|x_t|, p), s \cdot \text{percentile}(|x_t|, p)) \]

Where \(s\) and \(p\) are hyperparameters.

4. Diffusion Models for Audio and Text Generation

4.1 Audio Generation

Diffusion models for audio often work in the spectrogram domain:

  1. Convert audio to spectrograms: \[ S = STFT(audio) \]
  2. Apply diffusion to the spectrograms: \[ q(S_t | S_{t-1}) = \mathcal{N}(S_t; \sqrt{1 - \beta_t} S_{t-1}, \beta_t I) \]
  3. Convert generated spectrograms back to audio: \[ audio = iSTFT(S_0) \]

4.1.1 Mel-Spectrogram Diffusion

Some models operate on mel-spectrograms for better perceptual quality:

\[ M = Mel(S) \]

4.1.2 Vocoder Integration

To improve audio quality, some models integrate neural vocoders:

\[ audio = Vocoder(S_0) \]

4.2 Text Generation

Diffusion models for text generation typically operate in a continuous embedding space:

  1. Map discrete tokens to a continuous space: \[ e = Embedding(tokens) \]
  2. Apply diffusion in this continuous space: \[ q(e_t | e_{t-1}) = \mathcal{N}(e_t; \sqrt{1 - \beta_t} e_{t-1}, \beta_t I) \]
  3. Map generated continuous vectors back to discrete tokens: \[ tokens = argmax(Linear(e_0)) \]

4.2.1 Multinomial Diffusion

Multinomial diffusion directly models discrete token sequences:

\[ q(x_t | x_{t-1}) = (1 - \beta_t) I + \beta_t \frac{11^T - I}{K - 1} \]

4.2.2 Continuous Relaxation

Some methods use continuous relaxations of discrete distributions:

\[ x_t = \text{GumbelSoftmax}(logits, \tau) \]

Where \(\tau\) is a temperature parameter.

5. Techniques for Improving Diffusion Models

5.1 Progressive Growing

Progressive growing involves training the model on increasingly higher resolutions:

  1. Start with low-resolution data: \[ x_{low} = Downsample(x) \]
  2. Gradually increase the resolution during training: \[ x_{mid} = Upsample(x_{low}) \]
  3. Add new layers to the model as the resolution increases: \[ \epsilon_\theta(x_t, t) = [\epsilon_{\theta_{low}}(x_t, t); \epsilon_{\theta_{mid}}(x_t, t); \epsilon_{\theta_{high}}(x_t, t)] \]

5.1.1 Resolution-Dependent Noise Schedules

Some methods use different noise schedules for different resolutions:

\[ \beta_t^{(r)} = f_r(\beta_t) \]

Where \(r\) is the resolution level.

5.1.2 Adaptive Layer Normalization

To handle multi-scale generation, adaptive layer normalization can be used:

\[ \text{AdaLN}(h, r) = \gamma_r \cdot \text{LN}(h) + \beta_r \]

Where \(\gamma_r\) and \(\beta_r\) are learned parameters for each resolution level.

5.2 Classifier Guidance

Classifier guidance uses a pre-trained classifier to steer the generation process:

\[ \nabla_x \log p_\theta(x) = \nabla_x \log p_\theta(x | y) + \nabla_x \log p(y) \]

5.2.1 Classifier-Free Guidance

Classifier-free guidance extends this idea without requiring an explicit classifier:

\[ \epsilon_\theta(x_t, t, y) = (1 + w) \epsilon_\theta(x_t, t, y) - w \epsilon_\theta(x_t, t) \]

5.2.2 Guided Diffusion

Guided diffusion incorporates classifier guidance into the sampling process:

\[ x_{t-1} = \mu_\theta(x_t, t) + \sigma_t (\epsilon_\theta(x_t, t) + s \nabla_x \log p(y|x_t)) \]

6. Recent Advancements and Future Directions

6.1 Consistency Models

Consistency models propose a new framework for training diffusion models that allows for fast sampling:

\[ f_\theta(x_t, t) = x_0 + \sqrt{1 - \bar{\alpha}_t} \epsilon_\theta(x_t, t) \]

6.1.1 Consistency Training

The consistency model is trained to satisfy:

\[ f_\theta(x_t, t) \approx f_\theta(x_s, s) \quad \forall t, s \]

6.1.2 One-Step Sampling

Consistency models enable one-step sampling:

\[ x_0 = f_\theta(x_T, T) \]
